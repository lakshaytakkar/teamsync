Phase B Part 1: Product Catalog Browsing and CartThis is where the partner starts choosing the products they'll actually sell in their store. This catalog is completely separate from the Store Setup Kit in Phase A — that was about building the store, this is about filling it with merchandise.The product catalog should feel like browsing any modern B2B marketplace. When the partner opens it, they see products organized in a grid layout with filters on the left (or top on mobile). Each product card in the grid shows: a product image (or a placeholder category icon if no image), the product name, the category it belongs to, the price they pay us (this is the one and only price they see — never the EXW, never the landed cost breakdown, never our margin), the suggested MRP they should sell it for in their store (so they can mentally calculate their profit per unit), and a delivery speed badge.The delivery speed badge is critical — it's the ONLY way the partner knows where the product comes from, and even then they don't really know. Products sourced from our India suppliers show a green badge saying "Express: 3-7 days." Products sourced from China show a blue badge saying "Standard: 30-45 days." The partner picks based on how quickly they need it, not based on the supply chain. This is the same design principle we established earlier — one price, source is invisible, speed is the differentiator.Filters should include: category dropdown (showing the categories from our product database), delivery speed (express only, standard only, or both), price range slider, and a search bar that searches product name and category. There should also be a "sort by" dropdown: relevance (default), price low to high, price high to low, and "recommended for you" which shows products from categories matching their category mix preferences from onboarding. If they told us during onboarding that 25% of their budget goes to kitchen products, kitchen products should appear prominently when they sort by recommended.When the partner clicks a product card, a product detail page or detail panel opens showing: larger product image (or multiple images if available), full product name, description, category, their purchase price, suggested MRP, suggested store margin (MRP minus their price, shown as both rupee amount and percentage — "You make ₹70 per unit, 47% margin"), delivery speed with estimated date, minimum order quantity if any, and an "Add to Cart" button with a quantity input. Products flagged as "banned" for compliance should never appear in this catalog. Products flagged as "restricted" should appear with a warning note but still be orderable since restricted means "needs care" not "can't sell."The cart page collects everything they've added. It shows each item with: product name, price per unit, quantity, line total, and a remove button. They can adjust quantities here too. At the bottom: subtotal (sum of all line items), any applicable shipping or handling fee (a flat fee we can configure — or free above a certain order value), and the grand total. There should be a "Continue Shopping" button that goes back to the catalog and a "Proceed to Checkout" button.Load all product data from a mock JSON file — mockProducts.json — containing about 40-50 sample products across 8-10 categories, each with: id, name, description, categoryId, categoryName, partnerPrice, suggestedMRP, deliverySpeed ("express" or "standard"), imageUrl, complianceStatus ("safe" or "restricted"), minOrderQty, and inStock boolean. Also create mockCategories.json with 8-10 categories matching the products.The cart should persist in local state so it survives page navigation but not across sessions (if they log out and back in, the cart is fresh). The cart state should be managed through a service layer that's easy to connect to a real backend later. Show a cart badge with item count in the navigation so the partner always knows how many items they've queued up.This catalog must work great on mobile. Partners will browse products on their phones while sitting in their store space imagining what goes where. The grid should collapse to 2 columns on tablet and 1 column on phone. The filter panel should collapse to a filter button that opens a slide-over sheet on mobile.
