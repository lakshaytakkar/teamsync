Phase A: Store Setup Kit, BOQ Builder, and Layout GuideThis is what the partner uses during weeks 1-4 of their journey — after they've signed up and found a space, they need to physically build their store. This section helps them understand what they need, how much it'll cost, and gives them a visual sense of what their store should look like.The Store Setup Kit is a browsable catalog of everything needed to build a retail store — but these are NOT the products they'll sell. These are the fixtures, hardware, and infrastructure items. Organize them into these groups:Shelving and Display: gondola island shelving units (the freestanding double-sided shelves that go in the middle of the store), wall-mounted single-sided shelving (goes against walls), pegboard panels (for hanging items like tools, accessories), end cap displays (the shelving at the end of each gondola aisle — prime promotional real estate), dump bins (open bins near checkout for impulse items like batteries, candy, small accessories), and promotional display tables (for the entrance area showcasing featured products).Technology and Hardware: USB barcode scanner gun (the primary scanning device, this is the same one the POS billing screen uses), 58mm thermal receipt printer (the printer that prints the receipts we defined in POS Prompt 1), UPI QR code acrylic stand (a small stand that holds the store's UPI payment QR code at the checkout counter), and optionally a tablet or laptop (what the POS system runs on — we should recommend minimum specs).Counters and Furniture: billing counter desk (where the cashier sits — L-shape or straight, needs space for the laptop/tablet, scanner, printer, and a small area for bagging), backroom storage rack (for keeping buffer stock that doesn't fit on shelves), and staff stools.Branding and Signage: main store signboard for the exterior (the big sign customers see from the street), category aisle markers (small signs hanging above each aisle — "Kitchen," "Stationery," "Personal Care"), shelf price tag holders (the little plastic clips that hold price labels on shelf edges), banner standees for entrance and inside, and opening day promotional banner set.Lighting: LED tube lights (one per aisle section for even illumination), spotlight fixtures (for the feature wall or entrance display highlighting premium products), and basic electrical wiring and switch setup costs.Security: CCTV camera system (4 cameras covering the store floor, checkout, entrance, and backroom), and a cash drawer (the lockable drawer under the billing counter for storing cash — this connects to the cash register system we defined in POS Prompt 3).Each item in this catalog should show: the item name, a brief description of what it is and why the store needs it, a recommended quantity based on a 1000 sq ft store (these quantities should adjust if the partner entered a different store size during onboarding — for example, a 500 sq ft store needs 2-3 gondola units instead of 4-6), a price range in rupees (not a fixed price — these vary by city and vendor), whether it's "essential" (must have) or "recommended" (nice to have), and which group it belongs to.Load all this catalog data from a mock JSON file — mockSetupItems.json — with about 20-25 items across all the groups above. Each item has: id, name, description, group, priceRangeMin, priceRangeMax, recommendedQtyFor1000sqft, isEssential, buyLink (optional URL to a recommended vendor or Amazon listing), and imageUrl (optional).The BOQ Builder is where the partner creates their personal bill of quantities. They browse the setup kit catalog and add items to their BOQ, adjusting quantities as needed. The BOQ page shows a running list of everything they've selected with: item name, quantity they chose, estimated cost (quantity times the midpoint of the price range), and a running total at the bottom. They should be able to increase/decrease quantities, remove items, and see the total estimated setup cost. There should be a "Use Recommended" button that auto-fills the BOQ with all essential items at recommended quantities for their store size — one click to get a sensible starting point, then they customize.Store the BOQ in a mock JSON that persists in the browser's local state (or a mock service) — mockBOQ.json per store. The BOQ should be visible to our admin team later so we can see what each partner plans to buy and help them source.The Store Layout Guide is simpler than it sounds. We're not building a 3D designer. We're showing a recommended floor plan diagram for their store size. For a 1000 sq ft store, the layout should show: entrance zone (first 100 sq ft — promotional display table, dump bins, impulse products), main aisles (3-4 gondola runs with walking space between them), wall shelving around the perimeter, a feature wall (one wall section with spotlights highlighting premium or seasonal items), a checkout counter area (near the exit, with space for the billing setup and bagging), and a small backroom/storage area.This can be a well-designed static SVG or illustrated diagram that labels each zone with: zone name, what goes there, approximate dimensions, and which categories of products belong in each zone. Include 2-3 layout variants — one for 500 sq ft, one for 800 sq ft, one for 1000 sq ft — and show the one matching the partner's store size. If we don't have their store size, default to 1000 sq ft. This is educational, not interactive — the partner looks at it, understands the concept, and works with their local carpenter to execute. We can make it interactive later.There should also be a simple Brand Kit page showing: the recommended color palette for their store interior (warm whites, clean shelving, accent colors on signage), sample signage designs they can customize with their store name, and a few reference photos of what a finished store looks like (we can use placeholder images for now). This gives the partner confidence that their store will look professional.All of this Phase A content should be accessible even when the store status is "setup" — this is specifically FOR the setup period. Once the store is active, these pages remain accessible (the owner might want to reorder fixtures or reference the layout) but they move lower in priority in the navigation since Phase C becomes their daily workspace.
