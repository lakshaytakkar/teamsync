 The Billing Screen: Scan, Bill, Pay, PrintThis is the heart of the store — the screen the cashier uses for every single customer, all day long. Speed is everything here. A cashier scanning 5 items and collecting payment should take under 30 seconds. No unnecessary popups, no loading screens, no confirmation dialogs. Scan, scan, scan, charge, done, next customer.This entire POS section should only be visible when the partner's store status is "active" in our system. If their store isn't active yet, these pages should show a locked screen saying something like "Your store needs to be live before you can access billing and operations. Complete your store setup to unlock." Our ops team flips the store status to active when the store is ready — until then, POS is hidden behind that lock.The billing screen should be a clean two-panel layout that fills the full screen. The left panel is where the transaction happens. At the very top is a barcode input field — this is the most important element on the entire page. Here's how barcode scanning actually works in real stores: the USB barcode scanner gun is basically a keyboard. When you scan a product, the gun types the barcode number into whatever text field is focused and presses Enter automatically. So all we need is a text input field that's always auto-focused, and when it receives an Enter keypress, it looks up the barcode in our product database, finds the matching product, and adds it to the current bill. If the barcode doesn't match anything, show a quick inline error "Product not found" and clear the field for the next scan. Don't show a popup — the cashier needs to keep scanning without interruption. Next to the barcode field, add a camera scan button as a backup method — for when the barcode gun breaks or the sticker is damaged. This uses the device camera to read the barcode. Below the barcode field, add a quick search bar where the cashier can type a product name to find it manually. This is the fallback when scanning doesn't work — they type "kitchen organizer" and see matching products appear as they type, then click one to add it.Below the search is the current bill — a list of every item scanned so far. Each line shows: the product name, the MRP (selling price), the quantity (defaulting to 1 and incrementable), and the line total. The cashier should be able to tap plus or minus to change quantity on any item, or swipe/click to remove an item entirely. At the bottom of this list, show the total number of items and the grand total amount in a large, prominent font that's visible from across the counter.The right panel has two sections. The top section shows a grid of "quick add" tiles — these are the 8-12 most commonly sold products that the cashier can tap to add without scanning. Think of loose items or products whose barcodes get damaged often. These tiles show the product emoji or image, name, and price. Below that is a "held bills" indicator — we'll get to that in a second.At the bottom of the screen is a big prominent button that says "Charge ₹[total]". When the cashier taps this, a payment screen appears with three clear options: Cash, UPI, and Card. For Cash — show a field where the cashier enters the amount the customer handed over, and the system calculates and displays the change to give back. For UPI — the customer pays on their own phone (GPay, PhonePe, Paytm) and the cashier just taps "UPI Received" to confirm. We're not integrating with any UPI gateway at the store level — the store has their own UPI QR code and handles collections directly. For Card — same idea, the customer swipes on the store's card machine and the cashier taps "Card Received." After confirming any payment method, the sale is instantly complete.After a sale completes, show a receipt preview and a print button. The receipt should be formatted specifically for a 58mm thermal receipt printer — this is the standard small printer that every retail store in India uses, costs about 2000-4000 rupees. The receipt format is narrow, about 32 characters wide, with a clean layout: store name centered at the top in large text, store address and GSTIN below it, then a dashed line separator, receipt number and date/time on one line, payment method, another separator, then each item listed with its quantity and price, another separator, the grand total in large bold text, and finally "Thank you! Visit again" with "Powered by EazyToSell" at the bottom. Printing works through the browser's window.print() function — we just need a print-specific CSS stylesheet that formats the page to 58mm width with the right font sizes. The thermal printer is connected via USB and set as the default printer on that computer, so the browser just prints to it. No special drivers or integrations needed.One more critical feature: held bills. Sometimes a customer realizes they forgot something and steps away while the next customer is already waiting. The cashier should be able to tap a "Hold Bill" button that saves the current bill temporarily and clears the screen for a new transaction. A small badge shows "2 bills on hold" or whatever the count is. The cashier can tap "Recall" to bring back any held bill and continue it. Each held bill shows the number of items and the partial total so they can identify which one to recall. Held bills are temporary — they get cleared if no one recalls them within 24 hours.After every completed sale, the system needs to automatically deduct the sold quantities from the store's inventory. This happens in the background — the cashier doesn't see it or think about it. If an item being sold has zero inventory, still allow the sale (don't block it — the physical product is clearly in the store if the customer is buying it) but flag it internally so our stock records get corrected later.The database needs a sales table that records every completed transaction: store ID, a unique receipt number (auto-generated like R-0001, R-0002 counting up per store), the total amount, payment method, the individual line items with product ID and quantity and price for each, and a timestamp. It also needs a held bills table that stores temporary in-progress bills linked to the store. And it needs the beginning of an inventory table — each store's current stock per product, which this prompt initializes but the next prompt fully manages.This must work on a basic laptop or cheap tablet. Keep it light. No heavy animations, no complex layouts that break on small screens. The billing screen especially needs to be near-instant — every millisecond counts when there's a queue of customers waiting.
