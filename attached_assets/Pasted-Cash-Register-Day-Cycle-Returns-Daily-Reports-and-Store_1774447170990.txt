Cash Register Day Cycle, Returns, Daily Reports, and Store Settings
This prompt completes the store's daily operating system with the four remaining pieces: cash tracking, handling returns, business reporting, and store configuration.
Cash Register — this is directly inspired by Marg's "Daily Cashbook" and "Open/Close Cash Day" flow, but stripped down to what matters. Every retail store day follows a cycle: open the register with some starting cash, make sales all day, then close and count. Our system needs to track this.
When the store opens for the day, the cashier goes to the Cash Register page and taps "Open Register." They enter the opening cash amount — this is whatever cash was left in the drawer from yesterday plus any float they added. The system records this as the start of a new register session. Throughout the day, every cash sale automatically adds to the expected cash total. UPI and card sales are tracked but don't affect the cash drawer count. The Cash Register page should show a live running summary while the register is open: opening amount, total cash sales so far today, total cash expected in drawer right now (opening plus cash sales), and the number of transactions. When the cashier is done for the day, they tap "Close Register," physically count the cash in the drawer, and enter the actual amount. The system then shows: expected amount, actual amount, and the difference. Zero difference is perfect. Small differences (under ₹50) are normal rounding. Large differences mean something went wrong. The cashier can add a note explaining any discrepancy before confirming the close.
Each register session is saved as a record: store_id, opened_by, opened_at with timestamp, opening_amount, closed_by, closed_at with timestamp, closing_amount, expected_amount (calculated from sales), difference, status (open or closed), and any notes. Only one register session can be open at a time per store. The billing screen should show a subtle indicator "Register open since 9:15 AM" — and if someone tries to create a bill without an open register, gently prompt them to open one first (but don't hard-block it — if they dismiss the prompt, let them bill anyway, because a customer waiting at the counter is more important than process compliance). The store owner should be able to see a history of all past register sessions — date, who opened/closed, opening amount, expected, actual, difference — as a simple table. This is how the owner spots patterns, like a cashier who consistently has ₹200-300 short.
Returns — inspired by Marg's "Sale Return" but simplified heavily. Returns in a value retail store selling items under ₹500 are rare, maybe one or two per week. So this doesn't need to be fancy — just functional.
The flow: cashier opens the Returns page, looks up the original sale by typing the receipt number or picking from a list of recent sales (show the last 20 completed sales with receipt number, date, total amount). Once they select a sale, they see all the items from that bill. They check which items are being returned and can adjust the return quantity (maybe the customer bought 3 but is returning 1). They select a return reason from a dropdown: "defective product," "wrong product given," "customer changed mind," "damaged packaging," or "other" with a free text field. Then they confirm. The system does three things: creates a return record with all the details including which original sale it's linked to, adds the returned product quantities back to inventory (stock movement type "return"), and records the refund amount and method (cash refund or store credit — just record which one, we're not building a store credit redemption system right now). The return gets its own reference number like "RET-001" incrementing per store. The cashier can print a return receipt if needed, similar format to the sale receipt but clearly marked "RETURN" at the top.
Daily Sales Report — inspired by Marg's report engine but focused on what a small store owner actually looks at every evening. When they open this page, it defaults to today's data.
Top section shows four summary cards: total revenue today, number of transactions, average basket size (revenue divided by transactions), and average items per transaction. Below that, a section called "Top Sellers" — a table showing the top 10 products by quantity sold today, and next to it (or in a separate tab) top 10 by revenue generated. This tells the owner what's moving. Below that, payment method breakdown: how much came from cash, UPI, and card — shown as both rupee amounts and as a percentage bar so they can see the split visually. Below that, an hourly sales chart — a simple bar chart showing revenue per hour from store open to close. This reveals peak hours (usually 11am-1pm and 5pm-8pm in Indian retail) which helps the owner plan staffing. Below that, if there were any returns today, show a small returns summary: count of returns, total refund amount, top reasons.
The owner should be able to switch dates — a date picker defaulting to today. They should also be able to select ranges: "Yesterday," "This Week," "This Month," or a custom date range. When viewing a range longer than one day, the top summary cards show aggregated totals and the hourly chart gets replaced by a daily trend line showing revenue per day across the range. All numbers in Indian rupee formatting with proper comma placement (₹1,23,456 not ₹123,456).
Monthly view should additionally show: total revenue, total transactions, best day (highest revenue), worst day, average daily revenue, and a comparison to previous month if data exists ("Revenue up 12% vs last month"). This is the view the owner checks once a month to see how their business is doing.
Store Settings — a simple configuration page. The store owner can view and update: store name (as it appears on receipts and reports), store address (2-3 lines), GSTIN if they have one (just stored and printed on receipt, we're not doing GST calculation or filing), phone number, the default low stock threshold (how many units before a product turns yellow — defaults to 5), the list of quick-add products that appear on the billing screen (pick up to 12 products from a search), and a toggle for whether receipts should auto-print after every sale or just show a preview. Also show some read-only info: their store ID in our system, their partner package (Lite/Pro/Elite), and their store status (active/setup). This settings data gets used across all POS pages — receipt headers, alert thresholds, quick-add tiles.
One final important thing across everything in all three POS prompts: every piece of data belongs to us as well as the partner. We can see every store's sales, inventory, cash register sessions, and returns from our admin command center. When we build the admin analytics later, we'll aggregate this data across all stores to see network-wide trends, identify top-performing products, spot stores with problems, and drive smarter purchasing decisions. The partner sees their store only. We see everyone. Same database, different access. Build the data model with this in mind — every table should have a store_id so we can filter and aggregate.

That's the full POS system in three prompts. The depth comes from Marg — the item grid pattern, the stock movement audit trail, the cash day cycle, the returns-against-original-bill flow, and the reporting structure. But the complexity is stripped to what a small store actually needs. No accounting, no GST filing, no purchase management, no multi-branch inventory, no loyalty engine, no cheque books. Just: sell stuff, track stock, manage cash, handle the occasional return, and see how the business is doing tonight.
